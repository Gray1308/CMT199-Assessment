Who is Turing?

Alan Turing (1912-1954)

If you want to describe Turing and his life in the simplest words, you only need three words: war hero, homosexual, and creator (of computer science).

Winston Churchill once recorded in his memoirs that "Turing, as the hero who decippped the Enigma cipher machine, made the greatest contribution to the eventual success of the Allies in winning the Second World War."

Not only that, but Turing's brilliant achievements in mathematics and logic also set the grand blueprint for the development of modern computing, and the digital age in general. In recognition of his pioneering contributions, the Turing Award, named after him - the Nobel Prize of computing - was officially established in 1966 as the highest award in the field.

People usually only know him as a scientist, but they don't know that he was actually a world-class long-distance runner. Turing's best marathon time was 2 hours 46 minutes, only 11 minutes slower than his 1948 Olympic gold medal time. In a 10,000m race at an international track and field meet, he beat Richards, the Olympic silver medallist of the same year. Turing put a lot of effort into practising his long-distance running and finally reached world level.

Despite his extremely distinguished scientific contributions, he was given two options after his public trial and conviction because of his sexual orientation: prison or estrogen injection therapy (i.e. chemical castration). He finally opted for estrogen injections, which lasted for a year. During this time, the drug produced side effects including growing breasts, and also took a great toll on Turing, who had loved sport, both physically and mentally.

It may be that whenever we speak of Turing and the England that he saved, we cannot avoid remembering the darker side of history - the damage that people's arrogance and prejudice did to themselves echoing in our ears for decades like the chimes of Big Ben - but that is why we need to look at history and the contribution that those brilliant men and women made to humanity and use it as a lesson to ensure that civilisation does not lose its moral and human reins along the way.

Why is he so important for Computer Science?

To this day, his proposal for a universal Turing machine - an abstract computer that enables multitasking by changing software - has been affirmed as the precursor to contemporary computers, with common "ancestors" from the earliest cathode arrays to the various laptops we use today. Arguably, almost all of the later initial models, and even the earliest software and programs put into use, from the early Army's ENIAC electronic numerically integrated computer to Princeton's EDVAC to the ACE, were built on Turing's then little-known papers!

Although Turing himself built up the entire field of computer science almost single-handedly through the intelligent machines he created, he kept asking a question that even he had difficulty answering: how to define this new generation of "intelligence" that he created? What is it? What will it become? What are its possibilities? It was not until 1950 that his famous paper "Computer Mechanics and Intelligence" was published, which for the first time ever introduced the concept of "artificial intelligence", as well as the equally well known "imitation game" and the "The Turing Test, which was designed to determine whether computers would one day become like humans and actually think, and be able to fool those who ask them questions into thinking that the conversation is a human-to-human one.
