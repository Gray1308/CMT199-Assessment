What is computer thinking?

Computational thinking is the way of thinking by computer scientists, which underlies the bodies of knowledge in the computer science discipline. Computer science provides an intellectual foundation supporting the information technology industry, the worldwide digital economy and the information society.

What is my understanding of computational thinking?

Computational thinking is the way of thinking and the behaviour of people who think and analyse problems, build models and solve problems by using knowledge and concepts from computer disciplines. Computational thinking is a way of thinking that computer science students must master, but it is not unique to those studying or working in computer science; everyone should learn and apply this way of thinking. Computational thinking is much more than just programming computer programs; it requires us to think at different levels of abstraction. It is a computational concept that we use to think and solve problems, to deal with relationships and to live our daily lives.

The importance of computational thinking

In learning, the practice of computational thinking can help students develop the ability to continue learning, to try to solve complex problems from multiple perspectives, and even to ask new questions. In today's society, which tends to deliver information and short term solutions too quickly, mastering computational thinking can help us to solve problems more efficiently, make more attempts, and become focused and persistent in our learning, which are elements of the skills students should have.

At the same time, computational thinking can be applied to any function of a commercial business or public service. The ability to solve problems, analyse critically, communicate and think creatively, and to think beyond current methods of behaviour, are priceless skills in our competitive and ever-changing digital world.

Computational thinking runs through every aspect and function of a modern business, and it is beneficial to careers in virtually every sector, including consumer products, business and financial markets, energy, travel and tourism, or public services such as healthcare, education and law and order. Workplaces need employees to take an active role in thinking problems through and creating solutions. It has become more crucial in the 21st-century workplace where so much is now data-driven - analysing consumer behaviour, the movement in financial markets and the performance of public services, like health or policing, are just a few job roles that require. individuals to be able to think through problems in a way that a computer could understand.

Even if someone is not then in a position to create a solution using programming languages and computers, being able to understand and think through business problems using computation concepts is vital.
